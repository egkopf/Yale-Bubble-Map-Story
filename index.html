The Yale Coffee Ecosystem

By Ethan Kopf

Overview: The story that I aim to tell begins with asking the reader to consider the concept of Yale dominating the food and drink industry in areas of New Haven, with a case study on the “quick stop for coffee or a snack before or after a class”. With what frequency do Yale students choose to go to privately-owned, non-Yale coffee shops, where they may be surrounded by non-Yale-affiliated residents of New Haven, and served by non-Yale-employed people? And conversely, to what degree has Yale’s establishment of University coffee shops drawn students into Yale spaces who would have otherwise chosen to get their coffee or snack at a non-Yale space?

I have been inspired to analyze this concept of the Yale “Coffee and Snack” Bubble because of my own distaste for Yale’s inclusion of “points” on the partial meal plans. The restrictions on how these points can be spent have compelled me to get my coffee increasingly from the Elm so as not to lose the points, while I would prefer to enjoy time at and patronize independent organizations for coffee and snacks. I hope to show that Yale’s establishment of the Elm, Steep Café, and the Silliman Acorn have significantly impacted students’ decision making surrounding buying coffee and snacks. Added up, these small shifts in student behavior collectively strengthen the 'Yale Bubble' by drawing daily coffee and snack routines - normally opportunities for community interaction - into Yale-controlled spaces.

Hook: The underground café known as The Elm draws long lines of Yale students eager for coffee or a snack between classes. It’s a windowless café in a basement: what about the Elm would draw such crowds of people?

Beat C: How did student’s coffee and snack patterns change by 2017?

Moving into New Haven in 2017, with a spotlight on the Silliman Acorn and Steep Café. Primary focus on the proximity to Yale classroom buildings, to demonstrate that the closest coffee shop to so many students as they leave or enter a class has changed from a non-Yale-run coffee shop to now be a Yale-run coffee shop. Silliman Acorn and Steep Café are two Yale-owned cafés with different management structures, and I intend to supplement the geographic data with textual information about how Yale has used its advantage as a university to make these two options especially appealing to students: prices subsidized by the University and integration into the meal plan system.

Beat E: Broadly, what is the extent of Yale’s expansion into new food and drink markets?

Expanding the lens of the project to Yale’s shift away from the feeling of being a city school through development of civic-like spaces. Building the Well at the Schwartzman center indicates a new self-identity of Yale as a site for nightlife. The grab-and-go service from commons aims to capture the market for early/late lunches by extending hours to 11am-3pm. Butteries capture the late-night snack market.

Kicker: a feature on the massively long lines at the Elm. Intention is to show that despite having no windows, a sterile interior design, and average food and drink quality, the draws of the convenient location, exclusivity of the space to non-Yale affiliates and integration with the Yale meal plan are sufficient to turn crowds of Yale students inwards into the university for their coffee and snack needs.
